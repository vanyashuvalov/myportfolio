## Context

Biny is a freight-tech platform from Kazakhstan focused on logistics operations for shippers, carriers, dispatchers, and drivers. The driver app had to work for people who were much more comfortable with calls, paper, and WhatsApp than with a traditional app interface.

::: problemsolution
Problem | Drivers had to deal with a confusing MVP, low trust in digital payments, weak support feedback, and poor connectivity in real working conditions.
Solution | Redesign the driver experience around a simpler registration flow, clearer order states, better guidance, and an offline-first mental model.
:::

### Result

Created a driver app that focused on trust, clarity, and speed of action instead of feature density. The work combined field research, iterative design, and close collaboration with engineering over a long delivery cycle.

::: stats
- **200+** screens designed
- **50+** research sessions
- **20+** design iterations over 12 months
:::

## Research

#### Subject

Drivers in Central Asia usually work through a mix of phone calls, paper notes, and messenger chats. The product had to respect that behavior instead of forcing a desktop-style workflow into a mobile app.

The main constraints were:
- Limited digital literacy for older drivers
- Trust issues around unfamiliar payment flows
- Need for native-language support
- Poor network coverage in some regions
- High support dependency caused by unclear UI

::: callout
The challenge was to keep the driver focused on the next urgent action and make every step feel understandable at a glance.
:::

#### Process

There was a lot of operational complexity behind the product, so the research phase was focused on observing real behavior rather than only collecting opinions.

- Spent 3 days in the field with drivers
- Conducted interviews with 10 drivers
- Reviewed the existing MVP and support pain points
- Mapped registration, order handling, and payment flows
- Turned the findings into a list of concrete product hypotheses

::: stats
- **3** Days in the field
- **5** Design iterations
- **10** Driver interviews
:::

## Design

#### Prototype

The first prototypes were built to test the core journeys early: registration, order discovery, order acceptance, and payment understanding. The goal was to reduce cognitive load and make one main action obvious on each screen.

#### Simplified Registration

The biggest risk was registration, because if drivers could not get through onboarding, nothing else mattered.

- Reduced steps from 12 to 4
- Added clear progress indicators
- Added document upload with camera integration
- Added validation feedback and moderation status updates

::: callout
The app needed to guide the driver, not make him solve the product on his own.
:::

#### Order Management

The order flow had to be fast, readable, and tolerant of weak connectivity.

- Simple order search with basic filters
- One-tap order acceptance
- Clear status progression
- Push notifications for updates
- Offline order caching for unstable network conditions

#### Payment & Transactions

Payment flows needed extra clarity because trust was one of the biggest barriers to adoption.

- Clear payment status visibility
- Step-by-step withdrawal flow
- Transaction history with filters
- Bank transfer instructions
- Support guidance for payment questions

::: quote Long-haul Driver, Atyrau Region
Before, I would call support 5 times a day. Now I rarely need to. The app tells me everything I need to know.
:::

### Design Process

1. **Current Flow Analysis**: Mapped the existing MVP journeys and failure points
2. **Driver Interviews**: Validated pain points in real field conditions
3. **Hypothesis Testing**: Turned research into concrete product assumptions
4. **Wireframing**: Sketched fast, low-fidelity flows for the core tasks
5. **UI Design**: Built high-fidelity screens with a minimal, high-contrast system
6. **Prototyping**: Tested the most important scenarios before finalizing details
7. **Implementation Support**: Aligned with engineering on the shipped flows
8. **User Feedback**: Continued improving the product after release

### Technical Collaboration

The work was closely aligned with Android and backend development to keep the product usable in real field conditions:

- Offline-first behavior for unstable networks
- Camera integration for document uploads
- Push notification infrastructure
- Payment gateway integration
- Performance optimization for older devices

## Results

### Quantitative Metrics

::: metrics
- Support Tickets: 100+ daily → 20 daily (-80%)
- Registration Completion: <5% → 65% (+60%)
- Daily Active Users: 2x increase
- Order Completion Rate: +35%
:::

### Business Impact

::: stats
- **50,000+** drivers in the ecosystem
- **200+** screens designed
- **50+** research sessions conducted
- **20+** iterations over 12 months
- **Zero** driver churn caused by UX issues
:::

### Qualitative Feedback

The strongest outcome was not just better metrics, but lower anxiety during day-to-day use. Drivers started trusting the app enough to rely on it instead of defaulting to support calls.

### Future Improvements

- AI-powered scheduling recommendations
- Voice commands for hands-free operation
- Integration with truck telemetry
- Multi-language support for more regions
- Driver rating and rewards system

---

## Conclusion

The Biny Driver App showed that thoughtful UX in logistics is not about adding more functionality. It is about reducing uncertainty, making the next step obvious, and building trust through clarity.

The main lesson was the same across the whole project: simplicity wins. When the product feels understandable and reliable, even skeptical users can adopt a digital workflow with confidence.
